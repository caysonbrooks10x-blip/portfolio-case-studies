## The Client & Challenge

NovaTech had built a compelling B2B SaaS product — a project management platform specifically designed for engineering teams at mid-market tech companies ($10M-$100M ARR). Their product had strong NPS (62) and solid retention (89% annually), but their paid acquisition was hemorrhaging money.

They were spending $180,000/month across Google Ads and LinkedIn with a 1.2x ROAS. For a SaaS company at their growth stage, that meant they were essentially buying users at break-even while VCs were funding the gap. If they wanted to raise their next round on healthy metrics, they needed to get to 3x ROAS minimum.

The founder, Elena Vasquez, had a clear-eyed assessment: "Our product sells itself in demos. Our ads don't. We're paying for clicks and getting bounces."

My task: Build an ad copy system that could scale profitably — not just write better ads, but create a repeatable framework for generating ad variations that convert.

---

## Discovery & Strategy

### What I Found

I started with a 2-week audit:

1. **Search term analysis** — Pulled 6 months of search query data. Found that 73% of spend was on competitive generic terms ("project management software," "engineering PM tools") where they couldn't win on price or brand
2. **Landing page review** — All 12 ad groups directed to the same homepage. No relevance between ad copy and landing page experience
3. **Competitor ad analysis** — Analyzed 8 competitors' ad copy. Everyone was using the same generic benefit statements: "streamline," "collaborate," "ship faster"
4. **Historical ad performance** — Their best-performing ads historically were ones they stumbled into accidentally
5. **Customer interviews** — 8 interviews with paying customers. Key insight: they didn't sign up for "project management" — they signed up because their sprint planning meetings were a disaster and they needed to stop missing deadlines

### The Core Problem

NovaTech was advertising their category, not their differentiation. "Project management for engineering teams" sounds specific, but it's exactly what every competitor says. In a crowded market, generic messaging gets generic results.

### The Strategic Shift

We repositioned from **"project management software"** to **"stop missing sprint deadlines without adding more process."**

The target persona wasn't "engineering managers" as a demographic — it was "engineering managers who are stressed about delivery failures." Different psychological positioning, dramatically different ad copy.

## Implementation & Testing Protocol

I delivered a complete testing framework:

### Week 1-2: Foundation
- 12 ad groups live with 3 ads each (36 total headlines to test)
- Negative keyword list (500+ terms)
- Audience segmentation (by company size, industry, job title)

### Week 3-4: First Optimization
- Kill bottom 25% of ads
- Double down on top performers
- Refine audience targeting based on CPL by segment

### Week 5-6: Scale Winners
- Take winning ad themes, create 3-4 variations each
- Expand positive keyword lists in top-performing ad groups
- Test match types (exact vs phrase vs broad modifier)

### Week 7-8: Systematize
- Document winning patterns
- Create template library for ongoing ad creation
- Train in-house team on system

## Why This Worked

### 1. Problem-First Positioning
Generic "project management" ads got generic responses. "Stop missing sprint deadlines" got 3x the CTR because it spoke to a specific pain the target audience felt daily.

### 2. Search Intent Matching
Every ad group had dedicated landing pages. If someone searched "Jira alternative," they saw comparison copy — not the homepage. Quality Score improved, CPC dropped.

### 3. Negative Keyword Strategy
We cut 73% of spend on irrelevant queries. The remaining 27% was highly qualified traffic that actually converted.

### 4. Modular Copy System
Instead of writing one-off ads, I built a system that NovaTech's team could use to generate new variations indefinitely. The copy library had 40+ proven templates organized by audience segment and funnel stage.

### 5. Psychological Triggers
We tested urgency (trial availability), social proof (customer logos), specificity (exact setup time), and contrast ("not a PMO tool"). Over time, the data showed which triggers worked per audience segment.
