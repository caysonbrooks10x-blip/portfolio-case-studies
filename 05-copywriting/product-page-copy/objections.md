# Objections & Responses: Product Page Conversion Copy
## Lumina Skincare Case Study

---

## Objection 1: "Our brand is clinical — this copy sounds too emotional."

**Response:**
"Clinical and emotional aren't opposites. The most effective clinical brands — think Oшистин, La Roche-Posay — combine scientific credibility with emotional resonance. The science establishes why it works. The emotional copy establishes why it matters. We can have both: the ingredient and the outcome. '15% L-Ascorbic Acid' (clinical) + 'Wake up your morning routine' (emotional). Your credibility comes from the clinical data, not the jargon."

---

## Objection 2: "We're worried about FTC compliance with outcome claims."

**Response:**
"All claims in the copy are substantiated by clinical data or independent studies. The '89% reported visible brightening' is from an independent clinical trial with documented methodology (n=120, 14-day study). We're reporting data, not making promises. Every specific statistic has a study behind it. For the FTC, that's the difference between a claim and an unsubstantiated claim."

---

## Objection 3: "We don't have enough reviews to do what you're describing."

**Response:**
"The review count matters less than the review quality. One specific review with real outcomes, a real name, and a real situation is more powerful than 50 'Great product!' reviews. We include a testimonial sourcing guide that helps you collect better reviews. Questions like 'What were you hoping the product would do?' and 'When did you first notice a difference?' naturally elicit specific, outcome-focused responses."

---

## Objection 4: "Our packaging already communicates the outcome — won't copy be redundant?"

**Response:**
"Packaging communicates at the shelf. Product pages communicate when the customer is actively considering purchase — often after seeing an ad, a review, or a recommendation. At that moment, they need more: what exactly will change, how quickly, and why this formulation specifically. The copy on the page does work the packaging can't: tell the full story, address objections, and create urgency."

---

## Objection 5: "Our products are too similar to competitors' — how do we differentiate in copy?"

**Response:**
"Most skincare copy describes what's in the bottle. Your differentiation comes from formulation specifics: the buffered formula that prevents irritation, the pH-balanced approach that cleans without stripping, the encapsulated retinol that releases gradually. These aren't generic claims — they're specific choices that create specific outcomes. The copy surfaces what makes your formulation different, not just what it contains."

---

## Objection 6: "We want to keep pricing above $80 — how does copy justify premium?"

**Response:**
"Premium pricing requires premium justification. Generic copy can't justify $85 for a vitamin C serum — it just creates sticker shock. Outcome-first copy with specific data ('89% saw visible brightening in 14 days') provides the value framework that makes premium pricing logical. The copy makes the price feel like a rational choice, not an expensive one."

---

## Objection 7: "We have international customers — does this copy translate?"

**Response:**
"The core principles translate: outcome-first, specific data, benefit storytelling. The specific phrases may need localization (idioms, cultural references to skin types, regional regulatory language), but the framework is universal. We can develop a localization guide that shows which elements are universal and which need cultural adaptation."

---

## Objection 8: "We want to A/B test — how long before we know results?"

**Response:**
"With 40,000 monthly visitors, a 7-day test with 50/50 traffic split gives statistical significance for CVR changes of 0.5%+ (at 95% confidence). For add-to-cart rate changes of 3%+, 3-4 days is usually sufficient. We build headline variants and CTA variants into the deliverable so you can test immediately upon launch."
