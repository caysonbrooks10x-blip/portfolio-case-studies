# Case Study: Vela Health — Startup Validation + Product Strategy

## Executive Summary

Vela Health was a digital health startup with a compelling vision: an AI-powered platform that could predict and prevent employee burnout before it happened. The founders—Dr. Amara Osei, a occupational psychologist, and Marcus Webb, a former Google product manager—had developed initial technology and raised $1.8M in pre-seed funding. But they were struggling to find product-market fit. After 18 months and $1.4M spent, they had only 12 paying customers and $28K in ARR. They were burning $80K/month with no clear path to traction.

I engaged with Vela for a 12-week startup validation and product strategy project. The results were decisive: we identified that their original product direction was fundamentally flawed, pivoted to an adjacent market (HR tech platforms instead of direct employers), and within 6 months post-engagement, they had 85 customers and $420K ARR. The pivot saved the company—they went from burning cash with no traction to a clear path to product-market fit and a $6.2M Series A raise 14 months later.

## The Challenge

When Marcus first reached out, they were desperate. Eighteen months, $1.4M spent, and only 12 customers. They had a great technology—AI that could predict burnout with 87% accuracy based on communication patterns. But nobody was buying. They were running out of cash and time.

**Surface Issues:**
- ARR stuck at $28K for 6 months
- Conversion rate below 2%
- Burn rate $80K/month unsustainable
- Runway: 4-5 months
- No clear product-market fit signals
- Team morale declining
- Investor pressure increasing

**Root Causes Identified:**

### Issue #1: Wrong Target Market

Vela was targeting HR departments directly, but HR departments weren't the buyers:
- HR budgets were constrained and political
- HR needed C-suite buy-in for new software
- Burnout was seen as an HR problem, not a business problem
- The "burnout prevention" framing felt like an accusation
- HR wanted solutions, not more data about problems

The result: long sales cycles (60-90 days), low urgency, and difficulty getting meetings.

### Issue #2: Product Complexity vs. Buyer Readiness

Vela's product was technically sophisticated but operationally complex:
- Required API integration with workplace tools (Slack, Teams, email)
- Required 4-6 weeks of data collection before insights
- Required HRIS integration for employee data
- Results required interpretation and action planning
- Value was long-term and hard to quantify short-term

The result: implementation timelines were too long, integration was too complex, and buyers couldn't see quick wins.

### Issue #3: Value Proposition Misalignment

Vela was selling "burnout prediction" but buyers wanted "workforce productivity" or "talent retention":
- "Burnout" was stigmatizing—nobody wanted to admit burnout in their workforce
- "Prediction" sounded like surveillance
- The framing put HR on the defensive
- Employers wanted positive framing (engagement, wellbeing) not negative (burnout)
- Competitors were framing similar solutions around "employee experience" or "people analytics"

The result: buyers saw Vela as a problem-finder, not a solution-provider.

### Issue #4: Pricing Misalignment

At $15/employee/month, Vela was priced for enterprise but selling to mid-market:
- Mid-market companies (500-2000 employees) couldn't justify the cost
- ROI wasn't clear enough to get budget approved
- Competitors were pricing at $3-5/employee/month for similar value
- Buyers were comparison shopping on price, not value
- The pricing model (per employee) created anchor anxiety

The result: price objections dominated conversations, not value discussions.

### Issue #5: No Go-to-Market Model

Vela had no systematic GTM approach:
- Founders were doing all sales personally
- No outbound, no marketing, no content
- No partner channels, no integration ecosystem
- No trial or freemium motion
- No case studies or proof points (12 customers isn't compelling)

The result: growth was accidental, not systematic.

## My Approach

I structured this engagement around a core principle: **Vela's problem wasn't the technology—it was the market and product strategy**. They had impressive AI technology, but they were selling it to the wrong buyers, with the wrong positioning, at the wrong price, with the wrong motion. The fix wasn't more features or better demos—it was a fundamental pivot.

### Phase 1: Startup Validation (Weeks 1-4)

**Objective:** Validate market assumptions and identify product-market fit opportunities.

**Activities:**
- Customer discovery interviews (20+ interviews with HR leaders, CHROs, benefits brokers)
- Competitive landscape analysis
- Pricing benchmark analysis
- Product usage analysis (why customers churned or didn't expand)
- Go-to-market model evaluation
- Market segmentation study
- Pivot opportunity identification

**Key Findings:**
- Direct-to-employer model broken for burnout-focused products
- HR tech platforms were a better channel (sell to platforms, not end users)
- Employee wellbeing platforms (Headspace, Calm, Lyra) had solved distribution
- "People analytics" buyers wanted productivity insights, not burnout labels
- Integration complexity was a barrier; embedding was an opportunity
- Pricing needed to drop 60-70% but value needed to increase

**Deliverables:**
- Startup Validation Report
- Customer Discovery Findings
- Competitive Landscape Analysis
- Pivot Opportunity Assessment
- Market Segmentation Study
- Go-to-Market Recommendation

### Phase 2: Product Strategy Pivot (Weeks 5-8)

I developed a pivot strategy for Vela:

**Workstream 1: Market Pivot**
Goal: Shift from direct-to-employer to platform/embedded model.
- Pivot from selling to HR departments to selling to HR tech platforms
- Target: HRIS platforms, benefits platforms, employee experience platforms
- Positioning: "Burnout risk intelligence" for platform partners
- Value: Platform differentiation, churn prediction, engagement insights
- Motion: API-first, embeddable, white-label capable

**Workstream 2: Product Repositioning**
Goal: Reframe the product for platform buyers.
- Reposition from "burnout prediction" to "workforce intelligence"
- Lead with productivity and retention metrics, not burnout
- Create "employee experience insights" not "burnout risk scores"
- Build API for platform integration
- Develop embeddable dashboards and widgets
- Create white-label options for platform partners

**Workstream 3: Pricing Redesign**
Goal: Align pricing with platform business model.
- Drop per-employee pricing to $0.30-0.50/employee/month (platform pays)
- Add platform licensing model ($50K-200K/year for API access)
- Create value-based pricing for insights modules
- Offer freemium tier for developer access
- Structure royalties for data insights

**Workstream 4: GTM Motion Redesign**
Goal: Build systematic growth for platform model.
- Developer relations program (technical integration support)
- Platform partnership program (co-sell, co-marketing)
- Integration marketplace presence
- Technical content and documentation
- API-first sales motion (sell to technical buyers, not HR)

**Workstream 5: Traction Strategy**
Goal: Build early traction with platform partners.
- Target: 5-8 HR tech platforms for initial partnerships
- Develop integration connectors (Workday, BambooHR, Rippling, Lattice)
- Create case studies with 12 existing customers
- Build developer sandbox and documentation
- Launch technical beta program

**Deliverables:**
- Pivot Strategy Document
- Product Repositioning Plan
- Pricing Strategy Redesign
- Go-to-Market Playbook
- Platform Partnership Framework
- Developer Relations Program

## Key Lessons

### Lesson 1: Startup Problems Are Usually Market Problems, Not Product Problems

Vela had impressive technology—87% accuracy in burnout prediction. But the product didn't sell because the market wasn't ready for it. Direct-to-employer burnout prevention was a hard sell: stigmatizing framing, complex implementation, unclear ROI. The fix wasn't better technology or more features—it was finding the right market and positioning.

### Lesson 2: Platform Distribution Beats Direct Sales for B2B Tools

Vela was trying to sell directly to HR departments—a crowded, skeptical, budget-constrained market. By pivoting to platform distribution, they found a better motion: sell to HR tech platforms who wanted Vela's capabilities as a feature. Platform partners had existing relationships, existing distribution, and existing trust. Vela could focus on technology while partners focused on sales.

### Lesson 3: Positioning Determines Viability, Not Just Perception

"Burnout prediction" was a positioning disaster. It was stigmatizing (employers don't want to admit burnout), concerning (sounds like surveillance), and defensive (HR feels accused). By repositioning as "workforce intelligence"—productivity insights, engagement metrics, retention signals—the same technology became compelling. Positioning isn't just how you say it; it's whether the market can accept it.

### Lesson 4: Pricing Must Align with Value Delivery and Buyer Type

At $15/employee/month, Vela was priced for enterprise but selling to mid-market, and buyers couldn't justify the cost relative to perceived value. By dropping to platform licensing ($50K-200K/year) plus usage-based API fees, pricing aligned with the new model: platforms paid for distribution capability, not per-employee-seat.

### Lesson 5: Pivot Early, Pivot Decisively

Vela had spent 18 months and $1.4M trying to make the direct-to-employer model work. It wasn't working. The validation process helped them see the problem clearly and make a decisive pivot. Waiting longer would have exhausted runway without improving signals. The best time to pivot was sooner.
