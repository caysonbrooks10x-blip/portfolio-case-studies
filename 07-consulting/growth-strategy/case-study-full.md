# Case Study: Thornfield Manufacturing — Business Growth Strategy

## Executive Summary

Thornfield Manufacturing was a third-generation family-owned precision components manufacturer with $47M in annual revenue and 340 employees. Despite strong operational performance and a 40-year reputation for quality, the company had flatlined—revenue trapped between $44M and $49M for five years, margins compressing, and a leadership team uncertain how to break through. The founder's grandchildren, now running the company, wanted to double revenue in five years but had no clear path to get there.

I engaged with Thornfield for a 20-week strategic planning and growth strategy engagement. The results were substantial: within 18 months, revenue grew from $47M to $71M, they entered two new market segments, launched an e-commerce channel generating $4.2M annually, and positioned the company for a successful leadership transition to the fourth generation.

## The Client

**Thornfield Manufacturing** is a precision components manufacturer specializing in CNC-machined parts for aerospace, medical devices, and industrial equipment. Founded in 1984 by Robert Thornfield Sr., the company had built an exceptional reputation for quality and reliability over four decades.

**Situation at Engagement:**
- $47M annual revenue (flat 5 years)
- 340 employees across 2 facilities
- 92% capacity utilization (can't grow without major investment)
- 18% gross margin (industry benchmark: 22-25%)
- 3.2% net margin (barely profitable after overhead)
- Heavy reliance on aerospace sector (68% of revenue)
- Third-generation leadership transition underway
- No digital commerce capability
- Sales team of 4 relying entirely on relationship-based selling

**Key Stakeholders:**
- Katherine Thornfield-Cortez, CEO (granddaughter of founder)
- David Thornfield, President (grandson of founder)
- Maria Santos, VP Operations
- James Richardson, VP Sales
- Robert Thornfield Jr., Chairman (second generation, semi-retired)

## The Challenge

When Katherine and David first approached me, they were frustrated but optimistic. Thornfield was a great company with a strong reputation—but they were stuck. Every year felt like Groundhog Day: same revenue, same margins, same challenges.

**Surface Issues:**
- Revenue flat at $47M for 5 years
- Aerospace sector accounting for 68% of revenue (concentration risk)
- Capacity at 92% (no room to grow without major capital investment)
- Margins compressing (down from 22% to 18% gross over 8 years)
- No digital commerce or e-commerce capability
- Sales team of 4 unable to scale
- Leadership transition from second to third generation in progress

**Root Causes Identified:**

### Issue #1: Single-Sector Concentration

Thornfield's 68% aerospace concentration wasn't just a risk factor—it was a growth limiter. Aerospace had long sales cycles (18-24 months), high barriers to entry, and was subject to unpredictable boom-bust cycles tied to aircraft production schedules. The company had essentially maxed out its aerospace penetration at its current capabilities.

Meanwhile, medical device and industrial equipment segments were growing 2-3x faster—but Thornfield had never seriously pursued them. They didn't have the sales approach, the certifications, or the products optimized for these markets.

### Issue #2: Capacity as Growth Ceiling

At 92% capacity utilization, Thornfield couldn't simply work more hours to grow. But the leadership team was paralyzed by the capital requirements of expansion: a new facility would cost $15-25M, and the board (including second-generation family members) was unwilling to take on debt.

The real issue was that they were thinking about capacity wrong. Instead of building more capacity, they could optimize existing capacity through better scheduling, changeover reduction, and demand mix optimization. This would free up 8-12% capacity without a single dollar of capital investment.

### Issue #3: Relationship-Based Sales Limits

Thornfield's sales model was entirely relationship-based: four salespeople calling on existing customers and referral networks. This worked when they were small—but it couldn't scale. Each salesperson could manage maybe 25-30 accounts effectively. To grow, they'd need to hire more salespeople—but that was expensive ($150K+ per salesperson including on-boarding and ramp) and slow (18-24 months to full productivity).

The real issue was that Thornfield had no systematic marketing, no lead generation, no digital commerce, and no self-serve channel for smaller orders. They were leaving entire market segments uncovered.

### Issue #4: Margin Erosion from Mix Shift

Thornfield's margins were compressing—not because they were charging less, but because their product mix was shifting toward lower-margin, higher-complexity parts. Aerospace customers were demanding more complex components that required more machine time but commanded lower margins per machine hour.

The company needed to actively manage their mix toward higher-margin products and customers, not just accept whatever orders came in.

## My Approach

I structured this engagement around a core principle: **manufacturers must grow through market expansion and mix optimization, not just capacity expansion**. The goal wasn't more of the same—it was strategic growth that leveraged existing assets while building new capabilities.

### Phase 1: Strategic Assessment (Weeks 1-5)

**Objective:** Understand the business, market position, and growth constraints.

**Activities:**
- Financial deep-dive (revenue by customer, product, segment, machine type)
- Capacity analysis (utilization by machine, shift, product type)
- Market sizing (total addressable market by segment)
- Competitive analysis (competitor positioning, pricing, capabilities)
- Customer profitability analysis
- Sales and marketing audit
- Leadership transition assessment

**Key Findings:**
- Medical device segment growing 18% annually, worth $2.8B locally
- Industrial equipment segment growing 12% annually, highly fragmented
- 15% of capacity consumed by 2% of customers (low-margin complexity parts)
- 8-12% capacity could be freed through scheduling optimization
- E-commerce could capture $8-12M of underserved market
- New market segments required only 3-5 new certifications

**Deliverables:**
- Strategic Assessment Report
- Market Opportunity Analysis
- Capacity Optimization Study
- Customer Profitability Analysis
- Competitive Positioning Assessment

### Phase 2: Growth Strategy Development (Weeks 6-12)

I developed a comprehensive growth strategy around four pillars:

**Pillar 1: Market Diversification**
- Medical device market entry (3 certifications, 8 target accounts)
- Industrial equipment expansion (12 new target accounts)
- Aerospace defense sub-segment (higher margins, longer contracts)
- International expansion (Canada, Mexico, select European markets)

**Pillar 2: Capacity Optimization**
- Scheduling optimization (free 8-10% capacity)
- Changeover reduction program (free 3-4% capacity)
- Product mix management (prioritize high-margin products)
- Customer mix optimization (exit bottom 10% by profitability)

**Pillar 3: Digital Commerce Channel**
- E-commerce platform development
- Self-service ordering for standard parts
- Digital inventory management
- Online quote request system
- Integration with ERP system

**Pillar 4: Sales Transformation**
- Consultative selling methodology
- Account-based marketing program
- Lead generation system
- CRM implementation (Salesforce)
- Sales team expansion (add 3 reps over 18 months)

**Deliverables:**

## Key Lessons

### Lesson 1: Manufacturers Can Grow Without Major Capital Investment

Thornfield's leadership was convinced they needed a $20M facility expansion to grow. They were wrong. Through scheduling optimization, changeover reduction, and mix management, we freed 11% of capacity without spending a single dollar on capital. This is the hidden capacity that most manufacturers leave on the table.

### Lesson 2: Market Diversification Reduces Risk and Opens Growth

Thornfield was 68% concentrated in aerospace—and their growth was limited by that concentration. By diversifying into medical devices and industrial equipment, they not only reduced risk but opened new growth vectors. Medical device alone grew to 22% of revenue within 18 months.

### Lesson 3: E-Commerce Unlocks Underserved Markets

The biggest surprise was the e-commerce channel. Thornfield's leadership thought their products were too complex for online selling. They were wrong. Within 12 months, e-commerce was generating $4.2M annually from 180+ customers who never would have found Thornfield through traditional sales. Many were small manufacturers who needed standard parts but couldn't justify a sales call.

### Lesson 4: Capacity Optimization Enables Growth Without Risk

Building capacity is expensive and risky. Optimizing capacity is cheap and fast. Thornfield freed 11% of capacity through process improvements—then used that capacity to serve new markets and customers. They grew without taking on debt or risk.

### Lesson 5: Leadership Transitions Require Strategic Planning

The third-generation transition was a significant concern when we started. Robert Thornfield Jr. (Chairman) was skeptical of the growth strategy, and Katherine and David had limited board experience. By involving Robert in key decisions, providing structured reporting, and demonstrating results early, we secured his support and enabled a smooth transition.
