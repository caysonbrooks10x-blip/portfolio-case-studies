# HERO CASE STUDY: Apex Manufacturing — Business Growth Strategy
**$12M to $18.5M Revenue. 9-Point EBITDA Improvement. $24M Acquisition Offer.**
*Category: Consulting | Client: Apex Manufacturing | 16-Week Strategy Engagement*

---

## The Hook
A third-generation family-owned precision machining company stuck flat at $12M for four years. Aging customer base (22-year average tenure). 60% revenue concentration in top 3 customers. No sales function. Sixty-five percent capacity utilization. No digital presence. A 16-week growth strategy engagement later: $18.5M in revenue, 23 new customers including 4 Fortune 500 companies, EBITDA margin up 9 points, and a $24M strategic acquisition offer on the table.

---

## The Backstory

The owners of Apex Manufacturing had inherited a business, not built one. Their grandfather started it in 1962. Their father grew it to $12M. And for the last four years, it had gone nowhere.

The customers they had were loyal — dangerously loyal. The average customer relationship was 22 years. When those customers retired, sold their businesses, or shifted vendors, Apex had no pipeline to replace them.

The top 3 customers represented 60% of revenue. One of them was already reducing order volume. The CFO — who was also the founder's son — knew the concentration risk was existential. He just didn't know how to fix it.

They had no sales team. No marketing. No CRM. No website worth mentioning. Their "digital presence" was a Google Maps listing and a Yelp page.

And they had 65% capacity utilization — meaning a third of their manufacturing capacity sat idle every week.

---

## The Strategy Framework We Applied

Growth strategy for a mature industrial business isn't about "scaling." It's about diagnosis first.

Before recommending anything, we spent 3 weeks answering one question: **What's actually preventing growth?**

The diagnosis:

1. **No market positioning** — They competed on price for commodity machining. That's a race to the bottom.
2. **No go-to-market function** — No sales team, no CRM, no outbound, no digital marketing.
3. **No capacity discipline** — They took every order that came in, even unprofitable ones, because they needed the volume.
4. **No customer health monitoring** — No idea which customers were at risk until they left.
5. **Wrong target market** — Competing for SMB commodity work against shops with lower cost structures.

The prescription required all 5 to be addressed simultaneously.

---

## What We Built

**Phase 1: Market Repositioning (Weeks 1-6)**

We identified two adjacent markets with higher margins, longer contracts, and less price competition: aerospace precision components and medical device manufacturing.

Both required AS9100 (aerospace) and ISO 13485 (medical) certifications. Apex had ISO 9001. Getting certified would take 6 months — but it would open doors to contracts that couldn't be competed away on price alone.

We built the certification roadmap. They executed it. During the certification period, we built the pipeline infrastructure.

**Phase 2: Go-to-Market Function (Weeks 4-10)**

We couldn't hire a VP of Sales in 16 weeks and expect them to produce results. So we built the function ourselves and handed it to an internal champion.

- CRM setup: HubSpot configured for manufacturing sales process, with stages mapped to their specific quote-to-cash workflow
- Sales process documentation: 7-step process from prospecting to production handoff
- Outbound prospecting: We built LinkedIn outreach sequences targeting procurement managers at aerospace and medical device companies within 200 miles
- Inbound infrastructure: New website with case studies, certifications displayed prominently, quote request form
- Content: 6 case studies written from historical jobs, industry whitepaper on precision machining vendor selection criteria

The internal champion: a sharp operations manager who had been doing sales-adjacent work informally. We gave her the tools, the process, and the training.

**Phase 3: Capacity Optimization (Weeks 6-12)**

We audited every job running through the shop for 90 days. Found it immediately: 23% of jobs were running at negative margin. They were taking below-cost work to keep machines running.

We implemented a job-costing system with a minimum margin threshold. Any job below 22% gross margin required explicit approval from the owner before accepting.

Capacity utilization actually dropped — but profitability per machine hour went up 34%.

**Phase 4: Customer Health Monitoring (Weeks 8-14)**

We built a customer health score in HubSpot:
- Payment history (weight: 20%)
- Order frequency trend (weight: 25%)
- Order size trend (weight: 20%)
- Certification coverage (weight: 15%)
- Engagement score (weight: 20%)

Customers below 60 health score triggered a proactive outreach — a "check-in call" from the owner personally. Two at-risk customers were identified and retained within 60 days.

---

## The Breakthrough Moment

Week 12. The aerospace certification came through. Within 48 hours, the new website and LinkedIn outreach generated 3 inbound RFQs from aerospace companies.

The first one: $480K contract. Signed in Week 14. Margin: 31%. That single contract replaced the revenue they were about to lose from their top customer's reduction.

By Month 6: 4 Fortune 500 companies as customers. Zero competitive bidding on those contracts — they were sole-source or selected on capability, not price.

## What We'd Do Differently

**Bring in the CFO earlier for financial modeling.** We spent 3 weeks doing market research while the CFO had most of the data we needed on a spreadsheet. Should have started with 2 days of financial analysis.

**Build the case studies from historical work before the website launched.** We wrote them in 2 weeks under deadline pressure. They would have been stronger with more time and iteration.

**Address the owner's personal brand earlier.** The owner — a third-generation machinist — was the most credible voice in the industry. His LinkedIn profile had 87 connections. We should have started personal branding on Day 1.
